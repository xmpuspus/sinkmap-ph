Methodology & data

sinkmap.ph is a measurement of how fast the ground is moving under Philippine cities, from open satellite radar. It is not a forecast and not a verdict on any one place.

What this measures (and what it does not)

Subsidence rate only. The map shows measured vertical ground velocity in millimeters per year, 2016 to 2025, plus where that coincides with flood-prone ground. It does not predict which buildings will flood, does not assign blame, and does not issue per-building verdicts. What happens to any one place is a question for engineers and hydrologists on the ground.

How the rate is measured

  • Source: Sentinel-1 C-band radar (European Space Agency / Copernicus), descending track, ~40 acquisitions per city subsampled quarterly across 2016-2025.
  • Interferograms: short-baseline (SBAS) pairs processed on demand by Alaska Satellite Facility HyP3, then inverted to a displacement time-series and a velocity with MintPy.
  • Line-of-sight to vertical: radar measures motion along its line of sight. We convert to pseudo-vertical by dividing by the cosine of the incidence angle, under the vertical-dominant assumption (valid for groundwater, delta and reclamation subsidence; not for landslides). Single descending track; no full ascending+descending decomposition in this version.
  • Reference: velocities are relative to a stable, coherent reference point (InSAR has no absolute datum on its own). For Metro Manila that is bedrock-derived Sierra Madre piedmont.
  • Tropospheric correction: an empirical phase-elevation correction (no external weather model needed).
  • Map color datum: on the map, each city is colored relative to its own measured-area median (the standard relative-InSAR convention), so stable ground reads as stable and only ground that is measurably moving is colored. The validation rates below use the stable-reference datum.

Validation

No public downloadable PH subsidence raster exists to compare against, so each city is checked against the published maximum rate in Aslan et al. 2024 (Sentinel-1 InSAR + GNSS, 2014-2020). The pass condition is the same sign, location, and order of magnitude (within a factor of two).

The fastest sinking in Metro Manila is inland in the Bulacan/Pampanga lowland (around 15.18°N), consistent with the published maximum location. Cebu also shows a localized faster-sinking cluster (about 35 mm/yr) at the southern coast, consistent with coastal reclamation. Legazpi and Davao are coherence-limited: the radar signal over those small vegetated or upland areas is too decorrelated for a reliable rate with this method. They are reported as honest non-results, not forced numbers, and would need persistent-scatterer InSAR or a tighter urban area.

Flood coincidence (correlation, not causation)

Recent flood extents are derived independently from Sentinel-1 pre/post backscatter change (Typhoon Carina + monsoon 2024, 2025 monsoon, Typhoon Kristine 2024). The flood-prone baseline is Project NOAH return-period hazard. We then measure how much fast-sinking ground sits on flood-prone ground:

  • Metro Manila: 41% of high-subsidence ground is flood-prone, against 8% of all measured ground (about 5x).
  • Cebu / Mandaue: 18% against 2% (about 9x).
  • Iloilo: no preferential coincidence with the NOAH baseline (its chronic flooding may be drainage or tidal, outside a return-period depth model).
Disclaimer. This is observed spatial coincidence, not proof of cause. Subsidence is one of several flood drivers alongside rainfall, drainage capacity, tides, reclamation, and sea-level rise.

Surprising-findings layers (how the panel numbers are made)

The findings panel overlays patterns computed from the same time-series, with every number gated and baked into a data file, never hand-typed:

  • Acceleration. A per-pixel rate is fitted to the 2016-2020 half and the 2021-2025 half separately, each referenced to that half's own reliable-area median (so a reference or atmosphere drift that changes between halves cancels). The map shows the difference: red is sinking faster than before, green is slowing. The worst inland hotspot slowed (-96 to -79 mm/yr), yet more ground sped up (294 km2) than slowed (244 km2).
  • Differential tilt. The magnitude of the spatial gradient of the vertical velocity, smoothed about 400 m, in mm/yr per km. Uniform settlement is harmless; this gradient is what cracks roads and snaps pipes. Metro Manila reaches about 70 mm/yr per km at the steepest tilt.
  • Double exposure. Of the mapped buildings already on ground sinking faster than 15 mm/yr, the share that also sits inside a NOAH 25-year flood-prone zone (0.5 m or deeper): 46% in Metro Manila. Coincidence of two public layers, not causation.
  • By flood-hazard class. Fast-sinking ground concentrates in the Low and Medium flood tiers, not the deepest-flooding High tier, so flood depth and sinking rate are largely separate fields. High-hazard zones (river floodways) also have the lowest radar coverage.
  • Most-exposed town. Fast-sinking and fast-and-flood-prone area aggregated per OSM municipality boundary (by relation id, since Philippine place names repeat). San Miguel, Bulacan carries the most.

Error sources

Atmospheric (tropospheric water-vapor) phase delay; phase-unwrapping errors; DEM error; the choice of reference point; and the vertical-dominant assumption itself. Absolute rates are relative to an assumed-stable reference (no co-located GNSS benchmark). Coverage is limited to where the radar stays coherent: dense urban ground holds, vegetated and steep terrain decorrelates.
